A friend sent this to me just now. I was on a walk, desperate for answers of my own, tussling with this question: "Why do I have to *be* a person?" A tear runs between the two poles of my experience—nameless, peaceful, do-nothing dissolution on one side, and concentrated, connected orientation and attunement to the "other" on the other. And the seam that binds then is responsible for all of my discontent.

I responded to the friend.

![[Pasted image 20260621152808.png]]

It was obvious to me that the hiding of vulnerability is self-protection. Men are typically lest socially adept than women. Being socially ostracized, even on an individual level, often registers as an existential shock to the system. It's certainly something we want to protect against.

I continued walking.

As I was cresting the hill, I felt into the core of *my* issue. There's a tension in my chest and throat. It's this swirling thing, like a tornado, coalescing, but holding steady. In that moment, I was able to articulate it:

> There's this sense of wanting connection, wanting to love and *be*, and also feeling afraid. Also, there's a deep yearning to be free. It's the same yearning. I want to be free in connection. Maybe this isn't even about ego dissolution at all. It's just something I twisted around or kinked in my system so incredibly deeply that I can't even reach it. Not with words, not with sensation. It's just the substrate. It feels like something is twisted around. That connection *is* contortion, and freedom *is* disconnection. That's the cleanest I can describe it. It's one truth as encoded in my system.

I'm not sure how universal this is. It might be a subset of a lot of different attachment disorders (maybe I'll find out—I have [the book](https://www.amazon.com/dp/0393711528), after all; thanks, Harry). But, the fact that I was able to get to this feeling, breathe through it, sit and stay with it... that gives me hope. I have hope that these things are resolvable. They can be worked through. It's scary and hard, and it takes time, and it is at times incapacitating. And yet, if I can do it, maybe we can all do it. Maybe we can create a society that makes it its business to support and facilitate this work. Not with flaccid phrases and memes ("self care, therapy, healing"), but with actual structures and standards of culture.

Maybe.

> Connection is contortion, freedom is disconnection.

That's a lie. Maybe it's stitched into our culture, our minds, our very breath. But it's not *inherently true*. I'd like to disprove it. For myself and anyone else who feels it, too.
